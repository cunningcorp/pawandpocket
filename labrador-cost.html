How much does a Labrador really cost?

The sensible family favourite — predictable, but that appetite and those joints add up. Here's the honest 2026 breakdown.

Labradors are the classic family dog for good reason, and they have a reputation for being one of the more affordable big breeds to own. That's mostly true — but “affordable large dog” still means a real long-term commitment, driven by a big appetite and a couple of joint issues the breed is known for.

The short answer: a Lab puppy from a good breeder usually runs $800–$2,500 (around $1,400 on average), plus roughly $1,700–$2,900 a year to keep — putting the lifetime cost near $27,000–$35,000 across their 10–13 years. Not the priciest breed to buy, but the size and lifespan quietly stack up.

The upfront cost

From a reputable breeder, expect $800–$2,500 for a health-tested puppy, with working field-lines often at the lower end and show lines higher. Labs are also the most common breed in shelters, so rescue is a genuinely easy option here at $100–$500, usually with initial vet work included. Day-one setup — crate, bed, bowls, lead, first vet visit, neutering — adds roughly $500–$1,200, and it's worth budgeting for puppy training too, because a strong, bouncy 30kg dog that pulls is a genuine handful.

What tends to cost money with a Lab

Labs are generally sturdy, but a few predictable issues drive their lifetime bills — and most trace back to their build and their appetite:

  • Hip & elbow dysplasia — the classic large-breed joint problems, where surgery can run into the thousands per joint. Buying from a breeder who screens hips and elbows is the single best way to lower this risk.
  • Obesity — Labs are famously food-obsessed and pile on weight easily, and excess weight worsens their joints and shortens their lives, so portion control is a real cost-saver here, not just a nicety.
  • Ear infections — those floppy ears (and their love of water) trap moisture, so recurring ear issues are common and mean repeat vet visits.
  • Exercise-induced collapse & eye conditions — both are inherited and worth asking a breeder about; DNA-tested parents reduce the odds.
  • Later-life cancer & bloat — like most big dogs, Labs carry a real cancer risk as they age, and their deep chests put them at some risk of bloat, either of which can mean a large one-off bill.

The yearly running costs

  • Food — the big one for this breed. A hungry large dog runs roughly $500–$1,000 a year, and it's where their size hits your wallet hardest.
  • Routine vet care — check-ups, vaccinations, and prevention at about $500–$1,000 a year, with larger medication doses costing a little more than for a small dog.
  • Grooming — minimal; that short coat needs little more than regular brushing and the odd bath, so budget well under $100 unless you outsource it.
  • Insurance — reasonable for a large breed at roughly $35–$65 a month, reflecting the joint and later-life risks above.

All in, a typical year lands around $1,700–$2,900 — steady and predictable, until a joint surgery or an ageing-related bill lands.

Is insurance worth it for a Labrador?

Labs sit in the middle of the insurance question. They're not as medically complex as the flat-faced breeds, but hip and elbow surgery and later-life cancer are exactly the kind of large, unpredictable bills insurance is built for — and a single hip repair can equal several years of premiums. As always, the trick is to enrol while your Lab is young and healthy, before any joint niggle becomes a pre-existing exclusion. Our full take is here: is pet insurance worth it?
